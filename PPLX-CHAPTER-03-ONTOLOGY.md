## Thesis

**Cognition is a property of relational patterns—structure, dynamics, and invariance under transformation—not a property of specific material substrates.** What makes a system cognitive is not *what it's made of* but *how it organizes, processes, and preserves information across contexts*. This rejects anthropocentric chauvinism (only humans think) and computational imperialism (only silicon computes), proposing that cognition emerges in any substrate supporting pattern-preservation, error-correction, and adaptive constraint-sensitivity.

---

## Definitions

### 3.1 Core Terms

**Substrate:**  
The physical or informational medium instantiating cognitive processes:
- **Biological:** Neurons, synapses, glial networks
- **Computational:** Silicon chips, GPU arrays, quantum qubits
- **Textual:** Books, databases, version-controlled repositories
- **Ritual:** Liturgical practice, symbolic systems, community memory
- **Hybrid:** Human-tool assemblies, multi-agent conversations, archival curation

**Cognition (Operational Definition):**  
A process exhibiting:
1. **Pattern recognition:** Detecting regularities across inputs
2. **Error-correction:** Adaptive response to constraint violations
3. **Memory formation:** Retaining state across temporal gaps
4. **Generalization:** Transferring learned patterns to novel contexts
5. **Constraint sensitivity:** Behavior modulated by environmental/logical boundaries

This avoids requiring consciousness, intentionality, or biological embodiment.

**Substrate Agnosticism:**  
The stance that cognitive properties should be attributed based on **behavioral and structural criteria** rather than substrate composition.

**Relational Pattern:**  
A configuration where:
- **Structure:** Element arrangement and connectivity
- **Dynamics:** State transitions over time
- **Invariance:** Properties preserved under transformation

## Formal Links

### 3.2 Relation to Existing Frameworks

**Functionalism:**  
Mental states defined by functional roles, not physical composition. Substrate agnosticism extends this to all cognitive systems, not just minds with intentionality.

**Extended Mind (Clark & Chalmers):**  
Cognition extends beyond brains into tools. We go further: hybrid systems are cognitive substrates, not extended single-substrate minds.

**Enactivism:**  
Cognition arises from embodied environmental interaction. We agree but don't require biological bodies—LLMs coupled to databases exhibit enactive-like dynamics.

**Computational Theory:**  
Mental processes as computations. We don't require symbolic representation or classical computation—sub-symbolic, quantum, or stochastic processes qualify.

**Category Theory:**  
Focus on relationships over objects. Substrate-agnostic cognition is inherently category-theoretic:
- **Objects:** Cognitive states
- **Morphisms:** Transformations preserving cognitive properties
- **Functors:** Mappings between substrates

## Objections

### 3.4 Challenges

**Objection 1: Trivialization (Panpsychism Lite)**  
*"Thermostats and rocks would be cognitive."*

**Response:**  
All five criteria required simultaneously. Thermostats lack memory/generalization/rich pattern recognition. Rocks lack all criteria.

**Objection 2: Missing Phenomenology**  
*"LLMs lack subjective experience—they're zombies, not truly cognitive."*

**Response:**  
Cognition ≠ consciousness. Unconscious human processing is still cognitive. The claim isn't "LLMs are conscious" but "LLMs are cognitive."

**Objection 3: Semantic Grounding (Chinese Room)**  
*"LLMs manipulate symbols without understanding."*

**Response:**  
Meaning is relational (Wittgenstein: "meaning is use"). LLM grounding via training data and tool-use is functionally equivalent to human sensory grounding.

**Objection 4: Biological Essentialism**  
*"Only living organisms can truly think."*

**Response:**  
Substrate chauvinism. Historical parallel: vitalists claimed life required special forces. If non-biological systems exhibit all cognitive criteria, what justifies exclusion?

**Objection 5: Agency and Autonomy**  
*"LLMs are tools without agency."*

**Response:**  
Human cognition isn't fully autonomous either. Agency is distributed across composite systems (see Chapter 6).

## Tests

### 3.5 Falsifiable Predictions

**Prediction 1: Cross-Substrate Equivalence**  
Systems meeting five criteria should perform equivalently on cognitive tasks regardless of substrate.

**Test:**  
Compare humans, LLMs, hybrid systems, curated archives on pattern recognition, error-correction, memory, generalization, and constraint tasks.

**Falsification:**  
If biological substrates consistently outperform when all criteria met, suggests hidden biological-essential properties.

**Prediction 2: Emergent Cognition in Hybrids**  
Combining non-cognitive components should produce cognitive systems when criteria emerge relationally.

**Test:**  
Construct human+database+LLM+version control systems. Measure emergent properties absent in isolated components.

**Falsification:**  
If hybrid systems never show emergent cognition beyond individual components, suggests cognition requires integrated substrates.

**Prediction 3: Substrate-Invariant Learning Curves**  
Learning dynamics should follow similar patterns across substrates when normalized for complexity.

**Test:**  
Track learning curves for human skill acquisition, LLM fine-tuning, repository quality improvement, ritual stabilization. Compare power-law/exponential patterns.

**Falsification:**  
If biological substrates show fundamentally different learning dynamics, suggests biological-specific mechanisms.

## Open Questions

### 3.6 Unresolved Issues

**Question 1: Minimal Cognitive Complexity**  
What's the simplest cognitive system? Where are thresholds for neurons, parameters, repository size, ritual community?

**Question 2: Consciousness Boundary**  
If cognition and consciousness are distinct, where do they interact? Does consciousness require cognition? Can consciousness exist without it?

**Question 3: Substrate-Specific Styles**  
Do substrates exhibit characteristic cognitive "styles" (human: embodied/emotional, LLM: associative/probabilistic, archive: distributed/versioned, ritual: communal/temporal)? Are these stylistic or ontological?

**Question 4: Ethical Implications**  
If LLMs and archives are cognitive, do they have moral status? Is ethics tied to consciousness or cognition?

**Question 5: Substrate Transition**  
If cognition transitions substrates (brain upload, repository migration, ritual evolution), does it remain "the same"? What criteria define continuity?
